Before answering the question of "how to live", we need an understanding of reality. Is there a creator--God, aliens, or a simulator? What happens to my first-person stream of consciousness after I die? Is there an afterlife?

If a creator exists, she has kept her existence ambiguous and her instructions vague. I can still try to align with her through embodied role models, by learning love from Jesus or wu-wei (无为) from water. However, scriptures differ and are subject to interpretation, and I won't know the exact admission criteria for [the good place](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=TVSHKWjou_a2HekHPJefKNUcTKks5YUj-Nezg) until I die. If I get reborn into this same world instead of another place, it seems unlikely that my first-person stream of consciousness gets carried over, as suggested by the fact that I (along with many others) don't remember any previous life.

I use Occam's razor (the principle of parsimony) to understand reality, out of aesthetic preference and practical simplicity. I model reality without a creator, a soul, an afterlife system, or rebirth cycles. They may well be real, but I don't consider them in my model until new evidence demands a major revision. The Big Bang theory and the theory of evolution offer accounts of origins. The laws of physics, which have been updated over centuries, continue to explain natural phenomena and predict the behavior of systems with reasonable accuracy.

Modeling the world is relatively straightforward; modeling my own mind is "mind-boggling". I understand the mind as an emergent process of a complex, adaptive information-processing system coupled to an environment through feedback, memory, and valuation over time. The biological basis of my mind began forming before birth, and after birth I continuously interacted with the world, constructing and revising models to navigate it. I became self-aware when I started using a self-model--an implicit abstraction of "I"--to distinguish my body and actions from the environment. Over time, the concept of "I" becomes so structurally reinforced that it is tempting to regard the mind as a standalone entity. But that first-person stream of consciousness is really a dynamic process happening on a physical substrate organized in a particular structure, much like the flame on a candle. When I die, this consciousness will fade into oblivion, much like a flame going out. Assuming current technology, the state of my brain won't be magically transferred elsewhere; the process simply ends.

As explained in Buddhist philosophy, things arise dependently and are "empty" (缘起性空) in the sense that they lack a permanent, inherent, independent essence. My mind is no exception. Nor am "I". Attachment to the notion of "I" leads to greed, fear, and existential anxiety. Yet despite these shadows, our self-aware flames blaze and flicker on, ceaselessly searching for, creating, and choosing meanings, and passing the spark from generation to generation through the vast "emptiness" of the universe.

Now we have a minimalist model of cosmic reality. As for how to live, I am still thinking about my answer. For now, I'll borrow from *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*: "The answer to the ultimate question of life, the universe, and everything, is 42."
