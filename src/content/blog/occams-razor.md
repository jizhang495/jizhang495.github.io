Before answering the question of "how to live", we need an understanding of the reality. Is there a creator--God, aliens, or a simulator? What happens to my first-person stream of consciousness after I die? Is there an afterlife?

If a creator exists, she has kept her existence ambiguous and instructions vague. I can indeed try to align to her through embodied role models, by learning love from Jesus, or learning wu-wei (无为) from water. However, scriptures differ and are subject to interpretation, and I won't know the exact admission criteria for [the good place](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=TVSHKWjou_a2HekHPJefKNUcTKks5YUj-Nezg) until I die. If I get reborn into this same world instead of another place, it seems unlikely that my first-person conscious stream gets carried over, as supported by the fact that I (along with many others) don't remember any previous life.

I use the Occam's razor approach (the principle of parsimony) to understand the reality, due to aesthetic preference and practical simplicity. I model the reality without a creator, a soul, an afterlife system, or rebirth cycles. They may well be real, but I don't consider them in my model until new evidences demand a major revision. The Big Bang theory and the theory of evolution pretty much tackled the creation problem. The laws of physics, which have been updated through centuries, continue to explain natural phenomena and predict behaviors of systems with reasonable accuracy.

Modelling the world is straightforward; modelling my own mind is "mind-boggling". The mind is understood as an emergent process of a complex, adaptive information-processing system coupled to an environment through feedback, memory, and valuation over time. My mind probably started to form in my mom's womb sometime before I was born. After birth, I continously interacted with the world and iteratively contructed models to navigate it. I became self-aware when I started using the abstraction "I" to distinguish my body and actions from the environment. The concept of "I" becomes so structurally reinforced over time that it is tempting to regard the mind as a standalone entity, but that first-person stream of consciousness is really a dynamic process happening on a physical substrate organized in a particular structure, much like the flame on a candle. When I die, this consciousness will fade into oblivion, much like a flame going out. Assuming current technology, the state of my brain won't be magically transferred to somewhere else; the process simply ends.

As explained in Buddhist philosophy, because things arise dependently, they are empty of independent, fixed, self-existing essence (缘起性空). So is my mind. So am "I". Attachment to the notion of "I" results in desires, fear, existential anxiety, and incessant search for meaning, which make us human but also vulnerable.

Now we have a minimalist model of cosmic reality. As for how to live, I am still thinking about my answer. For now, I'll borrow from *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*: "The answer to the ultimate question of life, the universe, and everything, is 42."
