I kept putting off writing more posts as I was obssessed with writing something that "ages well". But I would have procrastinated indefintely if I aim for a well-researched, state-of-the-art essay worthy of everyone's read. "Completion before perfection" is always a good strategy. To reduce stress and lower the activation energy required, it is important to ditch perfectionism and embrace mediocrity.

## Comparison

People like comparing with others, and we want to optimize our lives to be the best version of ourselves. To provide some benchmarks for comparison and to define "perfect", "optimal", or "best", here are four people I consider successful:

<ol type="A">
  <li>The engineer: Member of technical staff in a lucrative tech company. The standard pathway to success and happiness. He may then marry a pretty wife and get many lovely kids.</li>
  <li>The scientist: <i>Nature</i> or <i>Physical Review Letters</i> &rarr; Professor &rarr; Fellow of the Royal Society &rarr; Nobel Prize. The ideal progression in my current trajectory.</li>
  <li>The entrepreneur: Founder and CEO of a company, which goes public on Nasdaq.</li>
  <li>The digital nomad: Passive income covers basic expenses. Freedom in time and location to create software, media content, literature, artwork, or music.</li>
</ol>

I've met many people in my life who are ahead of me in one of these four pathways. If I compare myself with them, I'd be inadequate. If I get ambitious and compare myself with Andrej Karpathy, Demis Hassabis, Elon Musk, or [@Jinnytty](https://www.youtube.com/@jinnytty_), I'd be a total failure.

Jealousy arises from the comparison with others, and regret arises from the comparison with an imaginary version of myself. If I majored in computer science, I could have become person A. If I worked harder towards a *Nature* publication, I could become person B. But I have ended up a mediocre researcher without even a *Nature Communications*.

Jealousy or regret makes me unhappy. Why should I compare anyways?

## Desires

The tendency to compare and the drive to become better off than others is called the "kiasu" spirit in Singlish. Competitions over benchmarks are zero-sum "status games" as explained in *The Almanack of Naval Ravikant*. Back in our hunter-gatherer days, high social status meant better access to food, protection, and mates. We are descendents of the more kiasu primates whose brain processes the feeling of "below" someone as a threat. Although most people have access to abundant food and shelter today, the ape in me still wants "more" in a lot of things, many of which are not entirely necessary.

My ape craves for more money, more recognition, higher Dota MMR, more followers on social media, more *Nature* papers, more citations on Google Scholar, higher h-index, more GitHub stars, more sex with prettier girls, more places travelled, more books read, more movies watched, more music listened, and so on. When I see other people fulfilling these desires more than me, I am invited to a status game where my ape wants to be on par or better.

But *I* am the boss; my ape isn't. *I* choose my desire. *I* define the benchmark. *I* select the game.

I don't have to compare with others in every racing track. I have every freedom to choose my racing track and leverage my kiasu spirit to achieve what I truly want. For instance, I play the sleep quality game. It is a very easy game to win! Just go to bed before 10 pm everyday (no later than 11 pm).

## Fixing the Past

Although I neutralize a lot of my ape's desires, some are indeed desirable, such as health. Suppose I can travel back to 2016 to tell myself to go for a dental cleaning and start flossing.

I hesitate. Would it really fix my past? Would it change me for better or for worse? When I found my periodontal problem in 2023, I compared myself to Shi Tiesheng (in a constructive way) and read some of his works. My receding gums have since then been a minor disability that prompted me to think about life, just like how Shi discussed an engineered life in *Good Luck Design* (《好运设计》). If I go back to remove my gum struggles, would I still make the same reflections? And it leads to a grandfather paradox: would I still travel back to give the advice in the first place?

If I subscribe to the multiverse theory, I create another version of myself in another universe. Is that person me? I don't think so. He is my clone, or fork. My current self in this universe remains unchanged.

I can't change the past. So I choose not to compare myself with the imaginary, healthier me. My ape likes this kind of "what if" distractions. I just accept whatever I have now and move on.

## Low Expectations

Jensen Huang said in the [2024 SIEPR Economic Summit](https://youtu.be/cEg8cOx7UZk?t=2170): "One of my great advantages is that I have very low expectations." With low expectations, we become resilient against setbacks, and we focus more on the process instead of the destination.

I lower my expectations by regarding myself as a mediocre person, which I indeed am according to many benchmarks. It is an incredibly liberating move, which led to the following vignette I co-wrote with Gemini:

> I walked into McDonalds and ordered a burger. Mediocre as I am, I'm eating this delicious burger! It is a miracle of modern civilization! Someone raised the cattle, someone baked the bread, and now I--a humble traveler--get to enjoy this explosion of flavor. I'm not just eating; I'm feasting.

Some may argue that I am wasting my intelligence if I don't translate it into a *Nature* publication or loads of money. I certainly want them, but instead of a detour, I can directly convert my intelligence into happiness through thinking and fulfilling my intellectual curiosity. At this point, prestige and wealth are merely side products.

## Cheers

Cheers to the engineer. Cheers to the scientist. Cheers to the entrepreneur. Cheers to the digital nomad. Cheers to my ape. Cheers to my past. Cheers to [the ample doses of pain and suffering](https://youtu.be/cEg8cOx7UZk?t=2291) in life. Cheers to [every little good thing in life](https://youtu.be/IILoTmIXd7s).

Cheers to my mediocre self!
